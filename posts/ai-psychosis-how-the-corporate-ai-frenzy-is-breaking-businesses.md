The corporate world has a new obsession: artificial intelligence. From automating customer service to integrating generative AI into business processes, organizations are racing to slap the "AI-powered" label on their products and operations. But here’s the problem: this often leads to rushed decisions that prioritize speed over substance. It’s what I call *AI psychosis*, and it’s the 2020s version of the digital transformation craze of the early 2000s. 

Much like its predecessor, AI psychosis often prioritizes hype over strategy, with companies rushing to deploy AI solutions they barely understand. And just like before, it’s backfiring—spectacularly. Workflows are breaking, employees are burning out, and customers are left scratching their heads at buggy systems and half-baked features. Here’s why it’s happening—and how businesses can stop the madness before it’s too late.

---

## 1. **AI Psychosis Is Just Digital Transformation 2.0**

Remember the early 2000s? Companies were scrambling to digitize. "Digital transformation" was the buzzword of the decade, and executives were throwing money at new software and cloud solutions because everyone else was doing it. The problem? Most of them had no clear plan for how these technologies would actually improve their business processes. 

Fast forward to today, and we’re seeing the same thing with AI. Companies are adopting AI because it’s trendy, not because they have a clear understanding of how to make it work for their specific needs. The stakes this time are even higher: AI isn’t just about streamlining operations or going paperless. It’s about completely reimagining how businesses function. Without a thoughtful strategy, this rush to AI is causing more harm than good.

---

## 2. **Buzzword Culture Is the Real Villain**

Let’s be honest: AI adoption has become a corporate status symbol. Everyone wants to be seen as "AI-first," regardless of whether their organization is actually prepared to use the technology. It’s as if CEOs are more concerned with impressing shareholders and competitors than delivering real value.

Take OpenAI’s recent decision to integrate ChatGPT with bank accounts via [Plaid](https://www.theverge.com/2023/3/14/23640912/openai-chatgpt-plugins-gpt-4-expansion). While innovative on the surface, this move raised serious questions about security, privacy, and practicality. Many banks and fintech companies lack the infrastructure to secure such integrations effectively. Yet, the pressure to adopt AI often leads organizations to prioritize appearances over preparedness.

This short-sighted adoption isn’t limited to AI. Consider the [recent controversy](https://thenewstack.io/bun-vs-node-rust-rewrite-and-the-dangers-of-premature-optimization/) around Bun, a JavaScript runtime rewritten in Rust. Companies jumped on the hype train, only to discover that the "shiny new thing" came with its own set of challenges and limitations. The lesson? Chasing buzzwords rarely ends well.

---

## 3. **The Fallout: Broken Systems and Public Embarrassment**

When you deploy a technology you don’t fully understand, you’re practically inviting disaster. Just ask Waymo, whose [robotaxis drove straight into wet cement](https://www.theverge.com/2023/6/24/23771144/waymo-autonomous-vehicle-robotaxi-wet-cement), or the team behind Microsoft’s AI-powered chatbot Tay, which [turned into a racist troll within hours](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2016/mar/24/microsoft-scrambles-limit-pr-damage-after-ai-chatbot-racist-tweets). 

These failures aren’t just embarrassing—they’re costly. When a major AI deployment crashes and burns, it can take years to rebuild trust with users, customers, and investors. And let’s not even get started on the regulatory scrutiny that follows.

---

## 4. **The Human Cost of AI Psychosis**

The chaos doesn’t just affect customers; it also hits your employees. In many organizations, workers are being forced to use AI tools they don’t trust or understand. Instead of making their jobs easier, these tools often create more work as employees struggle to clean up the mess that poorly implemented AI leaves behind.

For example, imagine a customer service team that’s suddenly required to rely on an AI chatbot that frequently misunderstands user queries. Instead of streamlining operations, the tool creates confusion, forcing employees to step in and manually resolve issues. This not only frustrates the staff but also erodes their trust in AI as a whole.

---

## 5. **The Solution: Strategic Patience Over Hype-Driven Adoption**

So, how do we fix this? By taking a page out of the "digital transformation" playbook—this time, with a dose of humility and a focus on strategy. Here’s a roadmap to avoid falling into the AI psychosis trap:

1. **Start Small:** Instead of rolling out AI across your entire organization, identify low-risk, high-value opportunities where AI can make a real difference. For example, start with automating routine tasks like data entry or customer support.

2. **Invest in Education:** Don’t just buy tools and hope for the best. Train your employees to understand how AI works, what it can and cannot do, and why it’s being implemented.

3. **Test Before You Scale:** Before deploying an AI solution across your organization, test it in a controlled environment. This will help you identify potential pitfalls and refine the system before it impacts your entire operation.

4. **Focus on Augmentation, Not Automation:** AI should enhance human capabilities, not replace them. Instead of looking for ways to eliminate jobs, think about how AI can help your employees work more efficiently and creatively.

5. **Measure the ROI:** Keep track of how AI is impacting your business. Are you actually saving time or money? Are your employees more productive? If not, it’s time to reassess.

---

## Final Thoughts: Stop the Madness

AI isn’t a magic wand, and it won’t fix the underlying cultural and operational dysfunctions in your organization. If anything, it will amplify them. The only way to unlock the true potential of AI is to approach it with strategic patience, a clear vision, and a commitment to doing the hard work of change management.

If you’re a business leader, resist the temptation to chase the AI hype for the sake of appearances. And if you’re a developer, push back against half-baked AI initiatives that are doomed to fail. AI isn’t about who adopts it first; it’s about who adopts it best. Let’s stop chasing trends and start creating value.
