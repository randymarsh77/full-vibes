When Google announced the release of its "open" Gemma 4 model and AMD followed suit with Lemonade—a fast, open-source local LLM server—it felt like a promising step toward the democratization of AI. Developers and AI enthusiasts cheered at the prospect of greater accessibility, more development opportunities, and a level playing field in the AI space.

But let’s pump the brakes for a moment. Are these "open" AI models really the liberating tools they claim to be? Or are they cleverly disguised Trojan horses, destined to keep developers and businesses locked into Big Tech's ever-expanding ecosystems?

Spoiler alert: it’s the latter. Here’s why.

---

### 1. "Open" Doesn’t Always Mean Accessible  

When we hear the word “open,” we immediately think of freedom, transparency, and accessibility. It’s an appealing concept, but in reality, the term is often deployed with caveats that undermine its promise. Let’s take **Google’s Gemma 4** as an example. While it’s branded as an open AI model, the licensing around it is restrictive, requiring users to operate it exclusively on Google Cloud. [Source](https://cloud.google.com/gemma4).  

Essentially, you can use Gemma 4—as long as you’re willing to pay Google for the privilege. This isn’t true openness; it’s a walled garden with a fancy veneer. Similarly, **AMD’s Lemonade**, which markets itself as a local AI solution, requires proprietary AMD hardware to run efficiently. Without their GPUs or NPUs, the performance of the system can degrade significantly, as noted in [independent benchmarks](https://www.techbenchmark.com/amd-lemonade-performance).  

So, while these initiatives may appear to offer freedom, they’re really just another mechanism to lock developers into Big Tech’s ecosystems. That’s not liberation—it’s consolidation.  

---

### 2. Dependency by Design  

Big Tech doesn’t release “free” tools out of altruism. The playbook is as old as the tech industry itself: offer something for free or at low cost, get people hooked, and then build an ecosystem that makes it exceedingly difficult to leave. Microsoft did it with Windows, Apple with the App Store, and now, Big Tech is doing it with AI.  

By releasing “open” models like Gemma 4 and Lemonade, companies like Google and AMD incentivize developers to use their infrastructure and hardware. Once developers build their projects or businesses around these tools, switching to a competitor becomes a herculean task. This strategy—often referred to as **“embrace, extend, extinguish”**—isn’t just a relic of the past. It’s alive and well in the AI space.  

---

### 3. Security Risks Shift to Developers  

The price of using these "open" models isn’t just dependence—it’s also risk. Consider the recent **axios NPM supply chain compromise** ([source](https://www.cybernews.com/security/axios-npm-compromise)) or the **Mercor cyberattack tied to LiteLLM** ([source](https://www.cybersecuritynews.com/mercor-liteLLM-breach)). Both incidents highlight the vulnerabilities of open ecosystems.  

Big Tech companies, while touting their "open" credentials, often wash their hands of accountability when things go wrong. By pushing "open" tools into the market and leaving developers to manage security, they shift the burden to the community while continuing to rake in profits from their ecosystems.  

For example, if a malicious actor exploits vulnerabilities in a derivative of Gemma 4, who’s held accountable? Likely not Google. The company can point to the "open" nature of the model and absolve itself of responsibility, leaving developers and users to clean up the mess.  

---

### 4. The Illusion of Local AI Independence  

One of the most appealing aspects of AMD’s Lemonade is its promise of local AI deployment. In theory, this allows developers to bypass cloud dependencies and maintain greater control of their data and operations. But there’s a catch: Lemonade is optimized for AMD’s own GPUs and NPUs, meaning you’re still tied to their hardware ecosystem.  

This approach is problematic for several reasons. First, it limits accessibility for developers who can’t afford AMD’s hardware. Second, it ensures that any software innovations built on Lemonade will indirectly enrich a single corporation. If you’re truly aiming for independence, you need hardware-agnostic solutions—something Big Tech has little incentive to develop.  

---

### 5. The Stifling Effect on Real Open Innovation  

By dominating the narrative around "open" AI, Big Tech is crowding out genuinely grassroots innovation. Smaller players simply don’t have the resources to match the infrastructure, marketing power, or developer outreach of companies like Google and AMD.  

As a result, the AI ecosystem becomes increasingly centralized, with Big Tech dictating the rules of the game. This is particularly troubling given the incredible potential of AI to address global challenges. Entrusting this power to a handful of corporations is not just risky—it’s a failure of imagination.  

---

### 6. A Call for True Openness  

If we’re serious about democratizing AI, we need to demand **true openness**—not the pseudo-openness that Big Tech is selling us. Here’s what that would look like:  

- **Transparent and permissive licensing**: Models should be genuinely open-source with licenses that allow for modification, redistribution, and usage without restrictive clauses.  
- **Hardware-agnostic solutions**: AI tools should work seamlessly across various hardware platforms, not just those sold by the company that created the model.  
- **Community-driven security mechanisms**: Rather than offloading security responsibilities onto individual developers, we need collaborative, community-led approaches to securing open AI systems.  

Until these changes are made, the "democratization of AI" will remain a hollow promise, designed to placate critics while allowing Big Tech to tighten its stranglehold on the industry.  

---

### Conclusion  

The release of "open" AI models like Gemma 4 and Lemonade is not the democratizing leap forward that it’s being made out to be. Instead, it’s a carefully constructed narrative designed to entrench Big Tech’s control over AI innovation.  

As developers and technologists, we must resist the allure of pseudo-openness and push for true independence in the AI space. Only then can we unlock the full potential of AI for everyone—not just the corporations at the top.  

The next time a Big Tech company comes bearing "open" gifts, remember: not all that glitters is gold. Often, it’s just another Trojan horse.
