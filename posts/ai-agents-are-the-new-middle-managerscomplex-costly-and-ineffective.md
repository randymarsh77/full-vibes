Agentic AI systems promise to revolutionize workflows with autonomous efficiency. But in practice, they often resemble digital middle managers—adding complexity, burning resources, and delivering diminishing returns. While their potential is undeniable, businesses need to approach these tools with caution and a critical eye.

Let’s explore why agentic AI systems, like those promoted by companies such as Cloudflare and Airbyte, might be creating more problems than they solve—and what organizations can do to avoid falling into the trap of digital bureaucracy.

---

## What Is Agentic AI?

Agentic AI refers to systems designed to operate autonomously, completing tasks that involve multiple steps and decision points without human intervention. These tools are marketed as the next frontier in automation, promising to handle everything from routine workflows to complex operations. However, as with any emerging technology, the reality is far more nuanced.

---

## The Illusion of Efficiency

Agentic AI is often touted as a game-changer for businesses. Take [Cloudflare’s AI agents](https://blog.cloudflare.com), for example, which autonomously create accounts, buy domains, and deploy services. It’s a futuristic vision that’s hard to resist. But here’s the catch: these systems frequently function as “black-box workflows,” where even developers struggle to understand their inner workings.

When something goes wrong, debugging becomes an uphill battle. Instead of streamlining operations, companies find themselves entangled in a web of convoluted processes. This lack of transparency mirrors the inefficiencies of human middle managers, with their penchant for vague job descriptions and overly complex reporting structures.

---

## The Compute Sinkhole

The promise of agentic AI comes at a cost—literally. Running autonomous agents can be prohibitively expensive. According to a recent report, **computational costs for these systems can be up to 45 times higher than using structured APIs** for similar tasks. The reason? These agents often rely on continuously running computationally intensive models, with little to no oversight for optimizing resource usage.

Consider [Airbyte’s autonomous agents](https://airbyte.com/), which facilitate access to multiple data sources. While convenient, chaining multiple agents together can lead to runaway compute costs. Each agent independently calls APIs, processes data, and communicates with others, creating a cascade of inefficiencies. It’s not unlike a team of middle managers who spend their days scheduling meetings instead of getting actual work done.

---

## The Accountability Black Hole

One of the biggest challenges with agentic AI is figuring out who’s responsible when things go wrong. Is it the developer who programmed the agent? The system architect who designed the workflow? Or the company that deployed the system?

This issue becomes even more critical in high-stakes industries like finance and healthcare. For example, [Anthropic’s exploration of AI agents in financial services](https://www.anthropic.com/) raises important questions: if an AI agent makes a flawed decision that costs a company millions—or worse, someone’s life savings—who is held accountable? Without clear lines of responsibility, the risks of deploying these systems may outweigh their potential benefits.

---

## Complexity Masquerading as Progress

Agentic AI systems are supposed to simplify our lives, but they often do the opposite. Much like the proliferation of human middle management, these systems can create additional layers of unnecessary complexity.

For instance, chaining multiple AI agents to complete a single task introduces a host of interdependencies and potential failure points. Each agent operates with its own logic, state, and potential for errors. When something breaks, you’re left chasing a trail of breadcrumbs through a tangled web of processes. This isn’t progress—it’s a recipe for frustration and inefficiency.

---

## The Strategic Blind Spot

The allure of automation can blind companies to the bigger picture. A [recent article on FullVibes](https://www.fullvibes.com/ai-strategies) put it succinctly: “When everyone has AI and the company still learns nothing.” Agentic AI systems often obscure inefficiencies rather than addressing them, making it harder for organizations to glean meaningful insights into their operations.

Instead of empowering employees, these systems frequently replace them—or worse, leave them cleaning up the messes the agents create. This not only undermines morale but also erodes trust in the technology itself.

---

## A Call for a Balanced Approach

It’s time to rethink the blind adoption of agentic AI. Instead of rushing to replace human workflows with autonomous systems, companies should focus on **augmentative AI**—tools designed to enhance human capabilities rather than replace them.

For example, rather than relying on autonomous agents to make high-stakes decisions, organizations could use AI to provide data-driven insights that empower humans to make better choices. This approach minimizes the risk of catastrophic failures and ensures that human judgment remains central to critical decision-making.

Transparency and accountability should also be non-negotiable. Businesses must demand clear documentation, robust oversight mechanisms, and measurable ROI before deploying these systems at scale.

---

## Conclusion: Don’t Let AI Become the New Middle Manager

Agentic AI systems are being hailed as transformative, but they risk becoming the digital reincarnation of middle management: an expensive, opaque, and often counterproductive layer that complicates rather than simplifies.

Before embracing these tools, companies must ask tough questions: Are these systems truly solving problems, or are they just adding unnecessary complexity? What safeguards are in place to ensure accountability? And most importantly, how can AI be used to empower, rather than replace, the human workforce?

By approaching agentic AI with healthy skepticism and a commitment to transparency, organizations can avoid the pitfalls of digital bureaucracy and unlock the true potential of automation. The future of work should be about collaboration, not replacement. Let’s build it wisely.
